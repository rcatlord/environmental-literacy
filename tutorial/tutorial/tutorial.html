Greenhouse gases

There are 3 main greenhouse gases: carbon dioxide (CO2), methane (CH4), and nitrous oxide (N2O). The heat-trapping abilities of these greenhouse gases are measured using an index called the Global Warming Potential (GWP). The GWP of each greenhouse gas is relative to carbon dioxide which is used as a reference point. For example, methane has a GWP over 100 years (GWP100) of 30. This means that measured over 100 years one tonne of methane can trap about 27-30 times the heat of 1 tonne of carbon dioxide.

Carbon dioxide CO2 1 1
Methane CH4 80 27-30
Nitrous oxide N2O 273 273

Methane therefore absorbs much more energy than carbon dioxide. However, the average lifetime of methane in the atmosphere is about 10 years. Compare that with carbon dioxide which can last for thousands of years. Nitrous oxide can remain in the atmosphere for more than 100 years, on average.

Global warming

The amount of carbon dioxide and other greenhouse gases in the atmosphere has increased rapidly since the start of the Industrial Revolution in 1750. Pre-industrial levels of CO2 were around 280 parts per million (ppm). Today they are over 50% higher. The latest atmospheric levels recorded at the Mauna Loa Observatory in Hawaii are 422 ppm (December 2023). These concentrations are higher than at any point in the last 800,000 years.

Source: NASA

The concentration of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere is directly related to changes in global temperatures. Ice core measurements from Antarctica show that carbon dioxide levels have tracked temperature changes through ice ages and interglacials.

Source: NOAA

The more greenhouse gases there are in the atmosphere the more they act like a blanket trapping radiant heat.
